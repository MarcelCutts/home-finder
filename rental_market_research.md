# London rental prices: East & North-East outcode guide

**E10 Leyton offers the best value at £1,550/month for a 1-bed**, roughly 20% cheaper than the priciest area (E15 Stratford at £1,950). Across all 9 outcodes, renters can expect to pay £1,750 for a typical 1-bed, £2,100 for a 2-bed, and £2,700 for a 3-bed—though new-build premiums in regeneration zones like Tottenham Hale and Stratford can push prices 25-30% higher. The data below reflects current asking rents from Rightmove, Zoopla, and rental aggregators as of early 2026.

## E3 Bow: Zone 2 value near Victoria Park

**Typical rents:** 1-bed £1,750 | 2-bed £2,200 | 3-bed £2,750

Bow offers strong value compared to neighbouring Bethnal Green (E2) and Hackney postcodes, with Zone 2 transport links via Bow Road and Mile End stations on the District, Hammersmith & City, and Central lines. Victoria Park sits on the doorstep, while Roman Road market (Tuesday, Thursday, Saturday) provides village-like character amid the East End. The area mixes Georgian terraces, post-war estates, and premium new-builds—with new developments like **Bow Green** (1,450 homes across 5 phases) and **Fish Island Village** (588 homes) pushing average prices higher.

**Value pockets** include older Victorian conversions near Roman Road and ex-council stock away from new developments; Fish Island canalside commands 15-25% premiums. **Watch-outs:** Properties near the A12 suffer traffic noise and air pollution, some large estates have variable quality, and cheaper offerings on busy main roads often have poor EPC ratings.

## E5 Clapton: Period charm at below-Dalston prices

**Typical rents:** 1-bed £1,750 | 2-bed £2,200 | 3-bed £2,600

Clapton remains **15-20% more affordable than neighbouring E8 (Dalston)** and N16 (Stoke Newington) while offering similar Victorian housing stock and strong community identity. The area transformed dramatically post-2012, with Chatsworth Road becoming an artisan hub featuring cafés, a French deli, and a revived Sunday market. Excellent Overground connections via Clapton and Homerton stations reach Liverpool Street in under 15 minutes, while Hackney Marshes, Millfields Park, and Springfield Park provide abundant green space.

**Best value** is found in Upper Clapton near Stamford Hill and around Clapton Park Estate, while Chatsworth Road properties command premiums. **Watch-outs:** New "Liveable Neighbourhood" traffic restrictions (August 2025) have created bus gates causing congestion on boundary roads—check location relative to these. The Clapton Park Estate's 1970s blocks vary significantly in quality.

## E9 Hackney Wick: Creative hub commanding premium rents

**Typical rents:** 1-bed £1,900 | 2-bed £2,400 | 3-bed £2,850

E9 commands the highest rents among Hackney postcodes due to its **Creative Enterprise Zone designation** and proximity to Queen Elizabeth Olympic Park. Freehold values have risen 154% since 2005, with annual growth (5.2% in 2024) outpacing the borough average. The area hosts one of the world's largest grassroots artist communities, with converted warehouses, canalside living, and a vibrant independent scene (Crate Brewery, Hackney Bridge). Hackney Wick Overground reaches Liverpool Street in 15 minutes.

**Value pockets** exist in older Homerton stock and ex-council properties, while warehouse conversions and waterfront new-builds (Fish Island Village, East Wick) cost significantly more. **Watch-outs:** Fish Island is technically Tower Hamlets (E3), not Hackney—different council tax rates and services. Historical flood risk near canals requires property-specific checks. Late-night venue noise affects some locations.

## E10 Leyton: Best value in the research area

**Typical rents:** 1-bed £1,550 | 2-bed £1,750 | 3-bed £2,600

Leyton offers **approximately 10% lower rents than neighbouring Walthamstow** and 15-25% below Stratford, making it the strongest value proposition among all 9 outcodes. The area attracts renters priced out of Hackney seeking comparable Victorian terraces at affordable rates. Named Time Out's "coolest London neighbourhood" in 2024, Leyton has developed a thriving independent food scene on Francis Road while maintaining quick Central Line access (12 minutes to Liverpool Street, 21 to Oxford Circus).

**Value hotspots** include the Francis Road area and streets near Newport Primary School, combining period character with good amenities. Proximity to Queen Elizabeth Olympic Park, Leyton Jubilee Park, and Hackney Marshes adds appeal. **Watch-outs:** Properties west towards the River Lea carry flood risk—check gov.uk flood maps. Parts of High Road Leyton have higher crime rates than the borough average. Waltham Forest requires selective landlord licensing.

## E15 Stratford: Elizabeth Line premium at London's transport super-hub

**Typical rents:** 1-bed £1,950 | 2-bed £2,200 | 3-bed £2,800

Stratford is the **most expensive outcode** in this assessment due to Olympic legacy regeneration and Elizabeth Line connectivity, which drove **29% rent increases** in the Maryland area alone. JLL research shows E15 experienced 24% rental growth since 2014, nearly double Greater London's rate. The area is London's second-most connected transport hub (Elizabeth Line, Central, Jubilee, DLR, Overground, Stratford International), with Westfield Stratford City and Queen Elizabeth Olympic Park on the doorstep.

**Value pockets** exist in traditional Victorian terraces around Stratford Village and West Ham—older stock on streets like Leggatt Road runs 15-20% below purpose-built towers but lacks amenities. **Watch-outs:** Parts near Stratford High Street fall within medium flood risk zones. The Carpenters Estate faces long-running regeneration uncertainty—avoid council stock facing potential demolition. Service charges on new developments add £150-400/month; scrutinize ground rent terms on leaseholds.

## E17 Walthamstow: Gentrified village with wide price spread

**Typical rents:** 1-bed £1,850 | 2-bed £1,850 | 3-bed £2,350

Walthamstow presents **dramatic price variation** between the premium "Walthamstow Village" area (rivalling inner London prices) and more affordable pockets in Higham Hill and around Wood Street station. House prices grew 5% last year, with Walthamstow Central (E17 5) seeing 11.2% growth. The area has transformed over 15+ years from affordable East End to cultural destination, featuring Europe's longest outdoor street market, artisan coffee shops, and independent boutiques.

Victoria Line from Walthamstow Central reaches King's Cross in 15 minutes. Lloyd Park and Walthamstow Wetlands provide green space, while God's Own Junkyard and the independent Empire cinema add creative character. **Watch-outs:** Build-to-Rent developments like The Eades offer 8 weeks rent-free (effectively 15% discount)—factor incentives into comparisons. Waltham Forest operates borough-wide selective licensing. Some estates like Marlowe Road are flagged for intervention.

## N15 South Tottenham: Victoria Line access at lower prices

**Typical rents:** 1-bed £1,600 | 2-bed £1,850 | 3-bed £2,600

N15 offers **15-20% savings versus neighbouring N16** (Stoke Newington), making it one of the better-value areas on the Victoria Line. The zone between Seven Sisters and South Tottenham stations provides the best balance of affordability and transport access—Seven Sisters reaches King's Cross in approximately 15 minutes. The area is undergoing gradual gentrification spillover from N16, with new developments (Vabel Lawrence, Apex Gardens) adding modern Build-to-Rent stock.

Multicultural character dominates, with Turkish and Caribbean restaurants along West Green Road and the vibrant Seven Sisters Market Latin American food hall. Green spaces include Markfield Park, Downhills Park, and Tottenham Green. **Watch-outs:** The Markfield Road area is an identified flood risk zone with £5m council mitigation investment underway. Haringey crime rate runs 14% above London average; violence and theft concentrate around transport hubs.

## N16 Stoke Newington: Family-friendly premium pricing

**Typical rents:** 1-bed £1,650 | 2-bed £2,150 | 3-bed £2,900

Stoke Newington's heavily gentrified status commands **15-20% premiums over N15/N17**, reflecting its "village feel" with independent shops, gastropubs, and excellent schools (Grazebrook, William Patten primaries). The Church Street core attracts the highest rents—prime houses near Clissold Park can reach £1,800/week. House prices grew 3.5% last year, with the market remaining buoyant due to family appeal. The **Woodberry Down regeneration** (5,500 new homes by 2035) may gradually help rental supply.

Outstanding green space includes Clissold Park with its deer enclosure, Abney Park Cemetery (restored 2024), and West Reservoir. Stoke Newington Overground reaches Liverpool Street in 13-19 minutes; Manor House tube (Piccadilly Line) is nearby. **Value pockets** exist towards the east near Hackney Downs and around Stamford Hill. **Watch-outs:** No direct tube access. Above-average property crime for Hackney. Higher rents mean increased competition for desirable properties.

## N17 Tottenham Hale: Regeneration creating a two-tier market

**Typical rents:** 1-bed £1,800* | 2-bed £2,300 | 3-bed £2,950

N17 exhibits the starkest **two-tier market** in this assessment. New Build-to-Rent developments (Heart of Hale, Hale Village, The Gessner, Windlass) command premium rents comparable to Zone 2 areas but include gyms, concierge, and WiFi—1-beds in these schemes run **£2,170-2,615**. Older Victorian conversions offer 20-30% savings but are increasingly scarce. Related Argent's £600m+ "Heart of Hale" regeneration is delivering 1,000+ homes through Q4 2026.

Victoria Line from the £30m-upgraded Tottenham Hale station reaches King's Cross in 12 minutes, with direct trains to Stansted Airport. Lee Valley Regional Park and Walthamstow Wetlands sit nearby, while the Tottenham Hotspur Stadium (2019) brought new restaurants and bars. **Watch-outs:** Tottenham Hale ward recorded 53 violence/sexual offences in August 2025 alone. Northumberland Park area remains challenging. Flood risk near waterways—£5m council mitigation investment ongoing.

## Data sources and methodology

Rental figures derive from current asking prices across multiple platforms, prioritized as follows:

- **Primary sources:** Rightmove and Zoopla rental listings (January-February 2026)
- **Aggregated data:** housesforsaletorent.co.uk, Propertistics, Home.co.uk
- **Supplementary:** ONS Private Rental Index, Foxtons N17 rental reports, JLL residential research
- **Area context:** Foxtons Local Life guides, Time Out area guides, Hackney Council/Haringey Council planning documents, GOV.UK flood service, CrimeRate.co.uk, Buildington regeneration tracking

All figures rounded to nearest £50 and reflect typical asking rents for standard properties—not achieved rents or premium new-build developments (except where noted). New developments with amenities typically command 15-30% premiums over these baseline figures. Borough-level ONS data (e.g., Hackney average £2,578/month, December 2025) captures achieved rents across all property types and tends to run 5-10% below portal asking prices.

## Key takeaways for value assessment

**Best value-for-money:** E10 Leyton and N15 South Tottenham offer the lowest rents with Victoria Line/Central Line access—expect £1,550-1,600 for 1-beds, roughly £200-350/month below area average.

**Premium areas:** E9 Hackney Wick, E15 Stratford, and N16 Stoke Newington command 10-20% premiums reflecting creative cachet, transport connectivity, or family appeal.

**New-build distortion:** N17 and E15 averages are increasingly skewed by Build-to-Rent developments—renters comparing like-for-like older stock will find 20-30% savings over headline figures.

**Regeneration watch:** Tottenham Hale (N17), Stratford (E15), and Hackney Wick (E9) face ongoing development pressure that will likely push rents higher through 2026-2027.
